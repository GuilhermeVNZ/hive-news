<p>Voyager Technologies, Inc. [NYSE: VOYG] and Infleqtion, a global leader in neutral atom-based quantum technology, announced a strategic partnership to advance dual-use quantum technology in low-Earth orbit and beyond. The collaboration marks a major milestone in the growing convergence of the quantum and aerospace industries. This announcement follows Infleqtion’s plans to go public through a merger with Churchill Capital Corp X [NASDAQ: CCCX].</p>



<p>“We’re bringing quantum utility out of the lab and into operational theater,” said Dylan Taylor, Chairman &amp; CEO, Voyager Technologies. “We’re unlocking a completely new class of dual-use capabilities with quantum timing, sensing and computing in space, strengthening the backbone of next-generation space infrastructure and ensuring mission continuity in increasingly contested domains.”</p>



<p>The two companies intend to demonstrate advanced quantum technologies, beginning with the integration of Infleqtion’s Tiqker Quantum atomic clock aboard the International Space Station (ISS) and then Starlab, the next generation space station platform designed to replace the ISS. The addition of an alternate high-precision, secure time source in space is expected to provide impact to commercial and national security missions, including those associated with Golden Dome, enabling autonomous spacecraft coordination and secure communications across constellations.</p>



<p>“The advantages of quantum technology multiply in space,” said Matthew Kinsella, CEO of Infleqtion. “In orbit, precision timing and sensing can improve navigation, enable new kinds of communication and make our critical infrastructure more resilient. We expect that our partnership with Voyager will demonstrate how quantum timing and sensing can enhance the reliability, scalability and security of space infrastructure, delivering real impact in space.”</p>



<p>Infleqtion’s quantum technologies have been at the forefront of quantum innovation for more than a decade, including contributions to NASA’s Cold Atom Lab, currently operating on the ISS. Building on this foundation, Infleqtion and Voyager will work to extend quantum capabilities into the commercial era, powering a new generation of spaceborne data, navigation and sensing applications.</p>



<p>Space-based quantum systems are emerging as a potential cornerstone of future infrastructure, and this collaboration represents one of the first concrete steps toward operational quantum infrastructure in orbit.</p>

<p>This week, we celebrate the birthday of the United States Marine Corps. For anyone who’s ever worn the uniform, this day hits home. It’s a reminder of service, sacrifice, and the drive to stay ready for whatever comes next.</p>



<p>I served ten years in the Royal Netherlands Marine Corps, which will soon celebrate its own birthday. We trained hard and operated in every kind of environment: cold, wet, and unpredictable. What I remember most isn’t just the missions, but the mindset. Marines are built to adapt. You plan, you prepare, and when the plan breaks, you find another way. That simple idea, Improvise, Adapt, Overcome, isn’t just a saying. It’s a way of life that shapes how you think and how you lead.</p>



<p>Those lessons have stayed with me ever since. At <a href="/">Infleqtion</a>, I work with teams building technology that carries that same spirit of readiness and precision. The next decade will redefine what it means to be ready for Marines and their allies around the world. Quantum technology will be part of that change.</p>



<p><a href="https://infleqtion.com/inertial-sensing/">Quantum sensing</a> will help Marines navigate and detect threats when GPS is jammed or unavailable. <a href="/quantum-rf-receiver/">Quantum communications</a> will secure critical information and keep teams connected when it matters most. <a href="/tiqker/">Quantum timing</a> and networking will improve coordination across land, sea, air, and space. And <a href="/quantum-computing/">quantum computing</a> and simulation will give commanders faster insights and help them make smarter decisions in the field.</p>



<p>That’s what excites me about the future. The same mindset that drives Marines—precision, adaptability, teamwork—is what drives quantum innovation. The tools may change, but the mission stays the same: protect, respond, and stay one step ahead.</p>



<p>As we mark the Marine Corps’ birthday and look ahead to the Royal Netherlands Marine Corps’ own celebration next month, I think about the Marines I served with. The long days, the quiet discipline, and the pride in doing something that matters. Serving taught me that strength comes from people and preparation.</p>



<p>At Infleqtion, we’re proud to support the people who serve. Our mission is to deliver technology that helps those in uniform operate with clarity, confidence, and courage in every environment.</p>

<p>A week after<a href="https://itsf2025.executiveindustryevents.com/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"> ITSF 2025</a>, Prague still feels like the perfect place to have talked about the future of time, a future that will be measured in picoseconds. The city has always had a relationship with precision, rhythm, and celestial order. Standing before the Prague Astronomical Clock, still ticking after more than six hundred years, it is easy to see why this city was chosen. That clock was once the height of technological ambition, combining astronomy, artistry, and engineering to give meaning to time itself. This year’s forum carried the same spirit, but with a new goal: to redefine how the modern world keeps time, connects systems, and stays in sync.  </p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full is-resized"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" width="472" height="629" src="https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Prague-Astronomical-Clock.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3166" style="width:350px" srcset="https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Prague-Astronomical-Clock.jpg 472w, https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Prague-Astronomical-Clock-225x300.jpg 225w, https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Prague-Astronomical-Clock-50x67.jpg 50w" sizes="(max-width: 472px) 100vw, 472px" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Prague Astronomical Clock</figcaption></figure>



<p>The discussions at ITSF made it clear that we have reached the limits of what satellite timing can do. GPS has served as the world’s common heartbeat for decades, but it was built for navigation, not for a world that depends on instantaneous communication and distributed intelligence. What comes next will be built on the ground, not in orbit. That is where <a href="/tiqker/">Tiqker </a>comes in, bringing quantum-enabled, optically stabilized time transfer from theory into practice. By replacing fragile radio signals from space with light through fiber, Tiqker synchronizes entire networks with astonishing precision, accurate to trillionths of a second. It is not just an upgrade; it is a revolution in how we define trust, security, and performance across global systems.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full is-resized"><img decoding="async" width="749" height="562" src="https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ITSF-Opening-Sessions-Max-Perez-Infleqtion.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3167" style="width:500px" srcset="https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ITSF-Opening-Sessions-Max-Perez-Infleqtion.jpg 749w, https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ITSF-Opening-Sessions-Max-Perez-Infleqtion-300x225.jpg 300w, https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ITSF-Opening-Sessions-Max-Perez-Infleqtion-50x38.jpg 50w" sizes="(max-width: 749px) 100vw, 749px" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">ITSF Opening Sessions</figcaption></figure>



<p>What stood out most during the week was the growing recognition that time itself is now a matter of security and independence. Countries and companies alike are beginning to treat time as a strategic resource. When communication networks, <a href="/national-security">defense systems</a>, and <a href="/finance/">financial platforms</a> depend on absolute accuracy, losing access to a reliable time source is not just inconvenient, it is dangerous. The shift to terrestrial and quantum-stabilized systems is about control and continuity. It ensures that when satellites fail or signals are compromised, the clocks on the ground keep everything running in perfect coordination.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full is-resized"><img decoding="async" width="521" height="391" src="https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ITSF2025-sessions.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3168" style="width:500px" srcset="https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ITSF2025-sessions.jpg 521w, https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ITSF2025-sessions-300x225.jpg 300w, https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ITSF2025-sessions-50x38.jpg 50w" sizes="(max-width: 521px) 100vw, 521px" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Quantum and Optical Clocks, as well as beer, are critical contributors to the Time and Frequency community!</figcaption></figure>



<p>The implications are enormous. Data centers spread across continents could soon operate as if they were a single machine. Telecommunications networks can maintain perfect synchronization for the next generation of services. Power grids, autonomous vehicles, advanced sensor networks, edge computing, and generative AI infrastructure all rely on this invisible precision, and Tiqker is building the backbone that makes it possible. The industry is moving from measuring time in milliseconds to governing it in picoseconds, because that is what the next phase of global communication and computation requires.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full is-resized"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="656" height="494" src="https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/INFQ-Ben-Weaver-ITSF.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3169" style="width:500px" srcset="https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/INFQ-Ben-Weaver-ITSF.jpg 656w, https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/INFQ-Ben-Weaver-ITSF-300x226.jpg 300w, https://infleqtion.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/INFQ-Ben-Weaver-ITSF-50x38.jpg 50w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 656px) 100vw, 656px" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Infleqtion’s Ben Weaver discussing quantum timing solutions with our colleagues</figcaption></figure>



<p>Looking back on the week, ITSF 2025 felt like a bridge between the mechanical past and the quantum future. The Prague Astronomical Clock was once a symbol of mastery over time. The systems discussed at the conference carry that legacy forward, giving humanity the ability not just to measure time, but to shape it with new levels of accuracy and resilience. <h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-why-nvqlink-matters"><strong>Why NVQLink matters</strong></h2>



<p>Scaling quantum computers requires <strong>logical qubits</strong> and fast quantum–classical feedback loops (for calibration, decoding, and error correction). Historically there wasn’t a single, high-performance way to couple QPUs to AI supercomputers—NVQLink provides that missing interface. It supports <strong>real-time orchestration</strong> of CPUs, GPUs, and QPUs, so quantum programs and AI programs can operate as one system.&nbsp;</p>

CUDA-Q exposes real-time callbacks across NVQLink, so developers can implement cycle-by-cycle control and hybrid algorithms without custom plumbing.&nbsp;</p>

<p><a href="https://infleqtion.com/">Infleqtion</a>, a global leader in neutral atom–based quantum technology, today announced a successful trial with the Royal Navy and MSubs, marking the first-ever deployment of a quantum optical atomic clock on an underwater autonomous vehicle. <p>This first-of-its-kind deployment took place aboard the Royal Navy’s new Excalibur (XCal) extra-large uncrewed autonomous vehicle (XLUAV), a pioneering testbed advancing the UK’s capabilities in autonomous underwater operations. Infleqtion is the first external technology partner invited to participate in the XCal program, reflecting the Royal Navy’s trust in the company as a proven quantum provider for national defense.</p>



<p>Unlike other vessels, submarines cannot rely entirely on Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) for timing and navigation. Traditional microwave-based clocks provide stability but accumulate drift over time, which can degrade accuracy in GPS-denied environments. High-precision local clocks like Tiqker, add to submarine capability by maintaining accurate timing and navigation autonomously, reducing reliance on external signals and improving overall resilience.</p>



<p>“This experiment was a first critical step towards understanding how quantum clocks can be deployed on underwater platforms to enable precision navigation and timing (PNT) in support of prolonged operations,” said Commander Matthew Steele, Head of Futures in the Royal Navy’s Disruptive Capabilities and Technologies Office. &#8220;The DCTO looks forward to championing further trials of quantum-based navigation technologies, such as Tiqker, onboard Excalibur as we seek to deliver quantum operational advantage for the Royal Navy.”</p>



<p>By placing a compact optical clock with performance equivalent to a national laboratory-grade time reference directly onboard a submarine, the trial showed how Tiqker can provide a steady “time heartbeat” smoothing out the noise that causes navigation drift. In addition, Tiqker provides a precise onboard reference for other critical submarine systems such as sonar, fire control, and secure communications, improving mission performance across multiple domains.</p>



<p>“By putting Tiqker directly on the Royal Navy’s Excalibur (XCal) testbed submarine, a next-generation autonomous platform, we are laying the foundation for fleets to navigate, coordinate, and operate with precision in any environment,” said Ryan Hanley, General Manager UK, Infleqtion. “We are grateful to the Royal Navy for their continued partnership on cutting-edge quantum technology trials accelerating adoption into naval operations.”</p>

<p>“Integrating a quantum clock onto XCal demonstrates how advanced timing can redefine what autonomous submarines are capable of,” said Matthew Troughton, Engineering Director, MSubs Ltd. “This trial points the way to platforms that can operate independently for longer durations, with the precision and reliability needed for future undersea missions.”</p>



<p>The Tiqker system represents a leap forward in compact, deployable quantum timing, building on Infleqtion’s broader portfolio of quantum sensing technologies.
