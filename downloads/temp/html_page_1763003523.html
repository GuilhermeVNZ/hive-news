<p>For production to be effectively brought back to the U.S., small and midsize enterprises need at least as much help as large automotive or electronics manufacturers. Justin Baucum, a U.S. Army Special Forces veteran, found the Isembard distributed approach to manufacturing to be just what he was looking for after a career in the military and doing consulting work.</p>
<p>&#8220;I was brought in as Employee No. 1 in the U.S. as a general manager for Texas,&#8221; he told <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>The Robot Report</em></a>. &#8220;I wanted to do something where I felt a sense of purpose like going back to my time in the military, where it&#8217;s very mission-centric.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;In February, I had a conversation with the founder, Alexander Fitzgerald, and he framed it as a diminishing supply of the Western manufacturing base and national capabilities,&#8221; <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/justin-baucum23/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Baucum</a> recalled. &#8220;I did a little research following that conversation, and I became alarmed by all the trends I saw at a macro level. The mission of Isembard to forge industrial acceleration was one thing that attracted me.&#8221;</p>

<h2>MasonOS connects machines and workflows</h2>
<p>London-based Isembard offers the proprietary MasonOS <a href="https://www.isembard.com/technology" target="_blank" rel="noopener">platform</a>, which it said can connect robots, five-axis <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/cnc/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CNC</a> machines, and automated workflows. The <a href="https://www.isembard.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">company</a> has designed its software to help cut costs, shorten lead times, and enable smaller <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/markets-industries/manufacturing/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">manufacturers</a> to get the benefits of franchise support rather than lose their independence.</p>
<div id="attachment_586001" style="width: 285px" class="wp-caption alignleft"><img decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-586001" class="size-medium wp-image-586001" src="https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Justin-Baucum-Isembard-copy-275x300.jpg" alt="Justin Baucum, leader of the U.S. arm of Isembard" width="275" height="300" srcset="https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Justin-Baucum-Isembard-copy-275x300.jpg 275w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Justin-Baucum-Isembard-copy-138x150.jpg 138w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Justin-Baucum-Isembard-copy-768x838.jpg 768w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Justin-Baucum-Isembard-copy-218x238.jpg 218w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Justin-Baucum-Isembard-copy.jpg 770w" sizes="(max-width: 275px) 100vw, 275px" /><p id="caption-attachment-586001" class="wp-caption-text">Justin Baucum, general manager of the U.S. arm of Isembard. Source: Isembard</p></div>
<p>&#8220;We&#8217;re aware of themes like reindustrializing and <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/?s=reshoring" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reshoring</a>, which are great concepts, but I hadn&#8217;t seen a lot of activity a few layers down, with people on the shop floor, getting their hands dirty making things,&#8221; said Baucum. &#8220;Isembard&#8217;s fist site had just gone live when I was interviewing, and now we have four facilities that are pretty much maxed out for capacity for the foreseeable future.&#8221;</p>
<p>He acknowledged that making complex things such as semiconductors is harder than simply programming a 3D model into a machine but noted that manufacturers of all sizes are trying to do more with less human input. Isembard&#8217;s goal is to free workers from time-consuming tasks such as checking documentation or material flows so they can focus on their craft and quality, Baucum explained.</p>
<p>&#8220;We want [the U.S.] to be the place that actually everyone sends the most difficult things to because we have the capability,&#8221; he noted. &#8220;It&#8217;s going to take some reps, but we&#8217;ve learned from those early stages, and we&#8217;ll just continue to refine as we go forward with more deployments.&#8221;</p>
<h2>Isembard franchise offers consistent service, market access</h2>
<p>While the franchise model is commonly associated with fast-food chains, it can be applied to help mom-and-pop machine shops across the U.S., said Baucum. Smaller manufacturers represent a fragmented market but can benefit from a distributed network, common operating procedures, service quality, and speed of delivery, he added.</p>
<div id="attachment_586003" style="width: 360px" class="wp-caption alignright"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-586003" class="wp-image-586003" src="https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Isembard-US-1-copy-226x300.jpg" alt="Isembard has opened a U.S. office in Dallas." width="350" height="464" srcset="https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Isembard-US-1-copy-226x300.jpg 226w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Isembard-US-1-copy-113x150.jpg 113w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Isembard-US-1-copy-768x1017.jpg 768w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Isembard-US-1-copy-180x238.jpg 180w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Isembard-US-1-copy.jpg 770w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 350px) 100vw, 350px" /><p id="caption-attachment-586003" class="wp-caption-text">Isembard has opened an office in Dallas to support U.S. franchisees. Source: Isembard</p></div>
<p>&#8220;If folks started a business, they wanted some degree of autonomy, but I think most will agree that we&#8217;re stronger together, particularly when going after larger customers or <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/markets-industries/aerospace/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">aerospace</a> and <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/markets-industries/defense-security/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">defense</a> contracts,&#8221; Baucum explained. &#8220;They&#8217;d retain ownership of an Isembard-branded facility but get all the benefits of our resources. It allows for our customer base, rather than trying to manage 100 individual relationships, to interface with a single brand and be certain that they&#8217;ll receive the same level of service.&#8221;</p>
<p>Services could include help with marketing, allowing franchisees to even out demand across the network and year, and eventually help them select and invest in automation.</p>
<p>&#8220;I ran a Chick-fil-A franchise and got soft skills from Amazon operations, which is the premier place to learn supply chain,&#8221; said Baucum. &#8220;With my background in the military, I have experience working to solve not just one little thing but to elevate everybody.&#8221;</p>
<h2>Veterans bring focus, teamwork to industry</h2>
<p>&#8220;There&#8217;s something called PCCs and PCI, or pre-combat checks and pre-combat inspections. One&#8217;s for equipment, and then one&#8217;s for the human beings,&#8221; he said. &#8220;And the concept is basically, when you are going to go out on a on a military operation, before you leave the last safe position in the field somewhere, you go through this exercise where you&#8217;re like, &#8216;Does everybody have their guns? Does everybody have all of their ammo? Do you have all the things that you need to be successful?'&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;When I was in leadership positions, I physically checked each person and touched the equipment,&#8221; Baucum added. &#8220;I trusted the people, but I was also doing my due diligence. The same thing will come into play as we launch franchisees. We&#8217;ll have a deployment team that will spend time before and after to make sure they&#8217;re up and running successfully. Folks will go through the learning curve at different rates, but we&#8217;re not saying, &#8216;Here&#8217;s our brand; we&#8217;ll send you work, and good luck.&#8217; It&#8217;s very much a partnership.&#8221;</p>
<p>Isembard plans to continue staffing up facilities in both the U.K. and U.S. to support its franchisees. Baucum will be speaking at a <a href="https://51vets.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">51 Vets</a> event this week, where he will share how the experience of veterans can benefit U.S. manufacturing.</p>
<p>&#8220;Every unit, regardless of whatever branch you&#8217;re in, you go through some sort of selection process, and then you have to pass your training, and then you get to be operational,&#8221; he said. &#8220;There was a moment in my selection process where I was physically crawling to continue forward momentum. That&#8217;s basically the sort of grit you need to have for doing something like making things.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;But in those moments, you can realize what the end goal is, whether you&#8217;re on the franchisee side and you&#8217;re building a business for yourself and your family, but it&#8217;s also part of something that&#8217;s larger,&#8221; said Baucum. &#8220;We&#8217;re bringing back a capability that has eroded in this nation, and then we&#8217;re also making things for like hypersonics or parts for <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/self-driving-vehicles/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">autonomous vehicles</a>, and you&#8217;re able to see those in real time.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;They&#8217;re on the front page of the news, and you&#8217;re like, &#8216;Hey, we we made that or made some portion of that,&#8217; he concluded. &#8220;I don&#8217;t think you find that in a lot of a lot of different businesses. There&#8217;s nothing wrong with having, like, a lawn care business, but it may be lacking in terms of having a big purpose. I think that&#8217;s very attractive to the veteran population.&#8221;</p>

<p>Flooding is a global challenge, but one company is developing robots to rise above the problem. Terranova Inc., which is using autonomous subsurface injection to lift and stabilize flood-prone terrain, this week emerged from stealth with $7 million in seed funding.</p>
<p>Flooding and land subsidence are driven by factors including <a href="https://www.climatecentral.org/press-release-flooded-future" target="_blank" rel="noopener">sea level rise</a>, weather extremes, groundwater withdrawal, soil consolidation, and the loss of natural watersheds. Flooding is <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2019/jun/20/us-rising-seas-defense-seawalls-cost-report" target="_blank" rel="noopener">estimated</a> to cost the U.S. economy over $180 billion each year, with over $68 billion required annually for infrastructure upgrades, according to Terranova. The San Francisco-based <a style="background-color: #ffffff;" href="https://www.terranova.inc/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">company</a> claimed that it is using technology to reshape the terrain itself.</p>
<p>&#8220;Having lived in San Rafael for 25 years, flooding has begun to seem like an existential problem,&#8221; stated Trip Allen, chairman of Terranova. &#8220;Much of the inner city is 6 ft. below sea level and floods regularly. I am proud to be bringing the first solution my city can afford to market.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;We&#8217;ve solved the challenges related to flooding &#8212; that&#8217;s the headline,&#8221; added Laurence Allen, CEO of Terranova. &#8220;We&#8217;re combining heavy robotics and geotechnical innovation to literally reshape the world. Terranova&#8217;s mission is nothing less than to terraform the earth and usher in a new era of resilience and societal abundance.&#8221;</p>
<h2>Terranova reshapes terrain with robotic injections</h2>
<p>Terranova reshapes terrain using robots roughly the size of cars that inject a wood slurry deep underground. This process lifts properties out of their flood zones with no surface disturbance, said the company. It asserted that this elevation provides proactive protection for all types of infrastructure, real estate, and coastal wetlands.</p>
<p>Terranova&#8217;s system consists of three injection robots and one &#8220;mothership.&#8221; It can lift up to one acre by a foot (0.4 hectares by 0.3 m) per day. The company said its approach enables &#8220;a step change in productivity&#8221; in comparison with conventional fill or civil work.</p>
<p>Terranova&#8217;s technology stack includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>A site suitability predictor based on soil composition and flood risk</li>
<li><a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/design-development/ai-cognition/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">AI</a> and machine learning reasoning models to design injection campaigns</li>
<li>Closed-loop control of semi-autonomous injection rovers to achieve the desired surface topology</li>
</ul>

<h2>Funding to grow engineering, field deployment teams</h2>
<p>Terranova said its seed round closed three times oversubscribed, bringing its valuation to $25.1 million. The company plans to use the funding to begin its first projects, reach full-scale production of its robot systems, and grow its core engineering and field deployment teams.</p>
<p>Outlander and Congruent Ventures led the round with participation from GoAhead Ventures, Gothams, and Ponderosa (a Galvanize Climate fund).</p>
<p>&#8220;Flooding and land subsidence are urgent global challenges,&#8221; noted Kevin Kopczynski, a partner at Congruent Ventures. &#8220;By turning waste biomass into a durable fill, Terranova creates both a climate adaptation and a climate mitigation solution at a much lower cost than traditional approaches &#8212; giving society a tool to push back.&#8221;</p>
<p>Terranova claimed that its process has demonstrated environmental safety and cost advantages over traditional flood infrastructure. At scale, the company said it is targeting costs that are up to an order of magnitude less than levees or seawalls.</p>

<p>Foxglove, a San Francisco-based startup building a data and observability platform for robotics companies, today said it has raised $40 million in Series B funding. The company has now raised more than $58 million since its 2021 founding.</p>
<p>Co-founders Adrian Macneil and Roman Shtylman previously worked at <a href="http://www.therobotreport.com/tag/cruise" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Cruise</a> and built a similar tool for the autonomous vehicle company. While at Cruise, they realized how few off-the-shelf development tools existed for robotics companies and decided to launch Foxglove.</p>
<p>Foxglove builds data and visualization tools that help robotics developers collect, analyze, and learn from the sensor data that their robots generate. The <a href="https://foxglove.dev/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">company</a>&#8216;s stated goal is to help build and deploy more reliable robots.</p>
<h2>Foxglove builds infrastructure to accelerate development</h2>
<p>Foxglove is working to bring the kind of in-house data infrastructure used by companies like <a href="http://www.therobotreport.com/tag/waymo" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Waymo</a> and <a href="http://www.therobotreport.com/tag/tesla" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Tesla</a> to the broader robotics industry without the need for hundreds of engineers.</p>
<p>&#8220;Our vision is to build the data stack and ML [machine learning] platform that every other robotics startup can use, so they don’t have to reinvent the wheel,&#8221; Macneil said. &#8220;We want to help every robotics company move faster.&#8221;</p>
<p>Foxglove&#8217;s platform is used by customers such as <a href="http://www.therobotreport.com/tag/amazon" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Amazon</a>, Anduril, <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/chef-robotics/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Chef Robotics</a>, Dexterity, <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/nvidia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">NVIDIA</a>, Shield AI, and a number of <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/self-driving-vehicles/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">autonomous vehicle</a> and <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/humanoids/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">humanoid robot</a> companies.</p>
<p>One of the more interesting customer uses, Macneil said, was Foxglove&#8217;s partnership with <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/shield-ai-raises-250m-scale-hivemind-enterprise-autonomy-developer-platform/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Shield AI</a>. It initially used Foxglove internally but later embedded the tools into its HiveMind autonomy stack, making Foxglove part of the software development kit (SDK) that Shield’s own customers use.</p>

<h2>Dexterity saves 20% in tooling time</h2>

<p>XTEND Reality Inc. has signed a multi-million-dollar, fixed-price contract with the U.S. Department of War, or DoW, to begin production of one-way attack drone kits. One-way attack drones are expendable unmanned aerial vehicles typically equipped with an integrated warhead. They can loiter in the air until they identify a target, and then be instructed to attack the target.</p>
<p>The <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/xtend/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">company</a>, which <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/xtend-secures-extension-series-b-scale-autonomous-tactical-robots/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">extended</a> its Series B fundraising in July, said its <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/swarm/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">swarm</a> technology enables a single operator to launch and control multiple <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/uav-drones/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">drones</a>. <p>“Our <a href="https://www.xtend.me/xos" target="_blank" rel="noopener">XOS</a> unifies sensors, radars, payloads, and third-party features and apps into a single AI-driven mission backbone,” said Rubi Liani, co-founder and chief technology officer. “This program extends that advantage deeper into complex terrain, scaling both precision and survivability through fully coordinated swarm behavior.&#8221;</p>
<h2>XTEND applies lessons from recent conflicts</h2>
<p>XTEND co-founder and CEO Aviv Shapira told <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><i>The Robot Report</i></a> that the company has directly incorporated lessons learned from recent conflicts in Gaza and Ukraine into its new drone swarm architecture. The architecture includes:</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><b>Safety and certification: </b>XTEND said its ESAD (Electronic Safe &amp; Arm Detonation Devices) high-voltage fuse-safety board approval is a key differentiator.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><b>Drone versatility: </b>The company has created different drone types for specific missions to address the limitations of standard FPV (first-person view) drones.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><b>Operational flexibility:</b> The new system allows a single operator to control multiple drones with different capabilities, using <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/design-development/ai-cognition/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">AI</a> pilots that can be configured for specific tasks.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><b>Tactical deployment: </b>XTEND developed a modular nest system that can be placed in various locations, making drone deployment more adaptable. It also offers a modular vertical takeoff and landing (VTOL) and munitions kit.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><b>Mission complexity: </b>The system can now execute complex missions with multiple drones working together, replacing human soldiers in dangerous scenarios.</li>
</ul>
<p>The nest can be delivered on the back of a truck and then be autonomously deployed by a remote operator who is monitoring the infield situation. This allows the drones to quickly get into the air and be able to support a given mission, said XTEND. Each nest contains a single drone.</p>

<p>According to Shapira, an AI pilot is a configurable software module with specific skills or capabilities that can be turned on or off for different drone missions. Examples include:</p>
<ol>
<li aria-level="1">An AI pilot that can find a rocket launcher and intercept it</li>
<li aria-level="1">A &#8220;follow the leader&#8221; AI pilot that can make drones follow a primary drone</li>

</ol>
<p>The operator configures each drone with the appropriate AI pilot depending on the mission at hand. The drones can use both hardened radio frequency communications and fiber-optic cable for communication in areas where enemy jamming is a potential obstacle.</p>

<p>Few companies have done more for robotics than iRobot since its founding in 1990. iRobot has been <em>the</em> household name in consumer robotics, putting more than 50 million Roomba robot vacuums into homes around the world. The Roomba brought robots out of the lab and into millions of homes, sparking curiosity, comfort, and even affection for machines.</p>
<p>iRobot&#8217;s PackBots have been used to keep U.S. military personnel safe by identifying and disposing of improvised explosive devices (IEDs), exploring caves and bunkers in Afghanistan and Iraq, and assisting in urban combat scenarios. PackBots were also used to explore the World Trade Center collapse after 9/11 and the Fukushima nuclear disaster in Japan.</p>
<p>iRobot also made a lasting impact on STEM education with its Create platform and <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/irobot-acquires-root-robotics-to-boost-stem-offerings/">Root educational robot</a>. I have great memories of my 11-year-old daughter coding with the Root robot when she was younger. These products helped inspire the next generation of roboticists and engineers.</p>
<p>Beyond its robots, iRobot alumni have gone on to do great things across the robotics industry and others.</p>
<p>Now, iRobot is in major financial trouble. The company that once defined consumer robotics is drowning in debt, battered by cheap competitors, geopolitical issues, and a <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/irobot-terminates-deal-with-amazon-laying-off-31-of-staff/">failed acquisition by Amazon that regulators killed</a>. The company <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/irobot-revenue-drops-again-with-no-sources-of-additional-capital/">said last week </a>that it &#8220;may be forced to significantly curtail or cease operations and would likely seek bankruptcy protection&#8221; if it can&#8217;t find additional sources of capital.</p>
<p>Saving iRobot might not make financial sense. But given how much money is being poured into <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/humanoids/">humanoids for the home</a>, cleaning up this iRobot mess is hardly the worst bet.</p>
<p>I’m reading Joe Jones’ book <em><a href="https://dancingwithroomba.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Dancing with Roomba</a></em>. Jones and others at iRobot pushed incredibly hard to create the original Roomba, succeeding where many others had failed. Even today, modern roboticists can learn many lessons from the early days of the Roomba and iRobot. The company was relentless at the start, doing anything necessary to ship robots. It pivoted its business many times. It faced repeated skepticism, funding challenges and technical hurdles. It withstood long development cycles and invented new technologies to solve a real customer pain point.</p>

<p><iframe loading="lazy" title="Pepsi ad #1, 2004" width="740" height="555" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/ksDfubARUB4?feature=oembed" frameborder="0" allow="accelerometer; autoplay; clipboard-write; encrypted-media; gyroscope; picture-in-picture; web-share" referrerpolicy="strict-origin-when-cross-origin" allowfullscreen></iframe></p>
</div>
<p>After going public on the back of the Roomba’s huge success, iRobot traded in its earlier relentlessness for caution and never found the next Roomba. <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/irobot-terra-robot-lawn-mower-delayed-due-to-covid-19/">It shelved a robot lawnmower</a> after 15-plus years of R&amp;D, undersold its military robotics division, and had other failed products.</p>
<p>Did you know: Quiet Logistics is a 3PL that used the Kiva Systems mobile robots. Quiet reached out to iRobot after Amazon pulled the Kiva robots off the market after acquiring the brand in 2012. Quiet Logistics had an idea for a robot it wanted iRobot to build. Bruce Welty, co-founder of Quiet Logistics, told us iRobot never responded to his outreach. The robot he wanted iRobot to build turned into Locus Robotics&#8217; LocusBot, which recently <a href="https://www.automatedwarehouseonline.com/locus-robotics-reaches-6b-picks-revenue-sets-records/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">surpassed 6 billion customer picks</a>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Nonetheless, iRobot&#8217;s decline these last couple of years has been tough to watch. Sure, buying iRobot right now might not look attractive on a spreadsheet. The balance sheet is ugly. The margins are thin. Revenue is declining (it made $681.85 million in 2024, a 23.44% decrease from 2023).<span class="uJ19be notranslate" data-wiz-uids="aBBXxf_a,aBBXxf_b" data-processed="true"><span class="vKEkVd" data-animation-atomic="" data-processed="true">&nbsp;</span></span> But some companies are too foundational to let fail. Saving iRobot isn’t about saving a product line. It’s about saving a legacy.</p>
<p>The robotics industry needs more iconic companies. And many iconic companies weren’t always financially stable. Apple nearly went bankrupt in 1997; Tesla faced financial trouble before its government loan; auto and airline companies have been bailed out multiple times; Marvel filed for bankruptcy in 1996; and Nintendo was near ruin before pivoting to video games in the ‘70s. There are countless examples of companies in situations like iRobot’s being saved.</p>
<p>iRobot has also become a pop culture staple, appearing on shows like <em>The Office</em>, <em>Parks and Recreation</em>, <em>Modern Family</em>, <em>The Simpsons</em>, and <em>Saturday Night Live</em>. It’s featured in countless memes and viral videos, and people have even named their Roombas.</p>
<p>iRobot isn’t just another struggling company. It’s a pillar of the robotics industry. Letting it disappear would be a loss far bigger than its current financial situation. Will anyone step up and save iRobot? Let&#8217;s give it a second chance to return to its relentless roots and once again pave the way for robotics.</p>

<h2>Connectivity is all-important for robotics</h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">It’s clear that commercial robotics has reached a point where it’s capable of solving a wide variety of use cases, and of meeting business and consumer expectations. But the surgical example in particular makes an important point: These advances are not driven by robotics alone.</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Communications <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/technologies/networking-connectivity/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">networks</a> have a <a href="https://newsroom.cisco.com/c/r/newsroom/en/us/a/y2025/m06/cisco-research-a-major-infrastructure-shift-is-underway-ai-could-double-the-strain-or-solve-it.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">crucial role</a> to play in ensuring that robots &#8212; whether <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/self-driving-vehicles/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">self-driving vehicles</a>, <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/uav-drones/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">drones</a>, <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/industrial-robots/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">industrial</a> or <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/collaborative-robot/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">cobot</a> arms, or <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/humanoids/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">humanoids</a> &#8212; can do a task consistently, reliably, and to a high enough quality standard that they are accepted. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">In a high-stakes world augmented or run by robotics, low-latency and ultra-reliable connectivity can be the difference between success and failure.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">A sudden drop in connectivity could force a robot to stop halfway through its task. Not all impacts are going to be equal &#8212; the loss of connectivity is less critical for drone delivery than for robot-assisted surgery. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Ongoing acceptance of and the social license to operate robots </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">is contingent on consistent, reliable performance. This also depends on a certain type of communications network. The industrial <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/iot/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Internet of Things</a> or IIoT is a significant underlying driver and enabler</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;"> </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">An important &#8220;North Star&#8221; characteristic for these networks is that they are lossless. All critical data is transmitted to and from the industrial robot so it can function optimally all of the time, without failure.</span></p>
<p><iframe loading="lazy" title="AI-driven industrial manufacturing: Real-time adaptive AI manufacturing with Denali AI" width="740" height="416" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/1vR4s4yS6aM?feature=oembed" frameborder="0" allow="accelerometer; autoplay; clipboard-write; encrypted-media; gyroscope; picture-in-picture; web-share" referrerpolicy="strict-origin-when-cross-origin" allowfullscreen></iframe></p>
<h2>Quality of connectivity service ties devices together</h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Hardware &#8212; routers, switches, fiber optic cabling, cellular base stations, and wireless access </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">points &#8212; is part of reliable connectivity. The other part is service assurance. Data traffic today is largely transmitted over the Internet, which itself includes a complex mix of connectivity services that are either owned by the robotics user such as the robotaxi company or hospital, or by another telecommunications provider.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">It&#8217;s important to have the capability to bring all those components and connections together and to make that communication lossless from one end to the other. Every part must work seamlessly and in synchronization to enable a robot to perform its intended tasks effectively. The integrity of the network connected to the robot has to be maintained, guaranteed, and assured at all times.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Today’s uses of robotics already demonstrate its widening applicability and potential. The question now is what can robotics achieve next? Key to unlocking more use cases will be trust in performance and in connectivity. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Both businesses and consumers need to trust that sensors, robots, and <a href="https://newsroom.cisco.com/c/dam/r/newsroom/pdfs/Cisco-2025-Networking-Research.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">AI</a> can complete the tasks at hand without disruption. To that end, IIoT and network assurance services play an integral part. The future is here, and so is the technology to make it a reality.</span></p>

<p dir="ltr">Physical world AI is the future for all autonomous machines, from cars and drones to tractors. The poster child for progress in this area is Waymo. Over many years, the company has developed cutting-edge onboard navigation technologies &#8212; including sophisticated hardware as well as numerous artificial intelligence and machine learning models &#8212; to guide its cars.</p>
<p dir="ltr">However, I don’t think onboard technology is going to be enough for us to have a world in which autonomous machines become ubiquitous. Unlike <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/waymo" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Waymo</a>, the vast majority of companies don’t have <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/waymo-raises-5-6b-to-accelerate-self-driving-car-growth/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-saferedirecturl="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.electronicsweekly.com/news/business/waymo-gets-another-5-6bn-2024-10/?utm_source%3Dchatgpt.com&amp;source=gmail&amp;ust=1762646807705000&amp;usg=AOvVaw1QlHdlWV31__Nk1_DONVJF">billions of dollars</a> to build the technology necessary for the compute engine to reside solely in the vehicle.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Rather, what’s needed are highly efficient cloud-based systems that, when combined with <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/design-development/ai-cognition/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">AI</a> models, provide an ultra high-precision representation of the planet so that <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/amrs/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">mobile</a> robots aren&#8217;t wholly dependent on onboard <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/design-development/mobility-navigation/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">navigation</a> systems. This is a future where autonomous machines will be able to optimize routes and, in some cases, see hazards in their path well before they embark on their journey.</p>
<h2 dir="ltr">The state of physical world AI today</h2>
<p dir="ltr">The AI that exists today is localized, with lots of processing on the edge or on the autonomous machine. What’s missing is AI that is aware of the broader physical landscape.</p>
<p dir="ltr">The good news is that there&#8217;s plenty of data about the physical world gathered from satellites, drones, and myriad other devices to feed these models. The bad news? As Gartner <a href="https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-07-09-gartner-says-earth-intelligence-is-a-20-billion-new-revenue-growth-opportunity-for-technology-and-service-providers-through-2030" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-saferedirecturl="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-07-09-gartner-says-earth-intelligence-is-a-20-billion-new-revenue-growth-opportunity-for-technology-and-service-providers-through-2030&amp;source=gmail&amp;ust=1762646807705000&amp;usg=AOvVaw3dCwqyJ8728M-3mCeLzulf">notes</a>, physical-world data typically needs heavy engineering to be usable by AI.</p>
<p dir="ltr">This is a field in which my company, Wherobots, and others are working. What we call the “spatial intelligence cloud” is technology designed to process disparate forms of physical world data. This includes abstract shapes such as vectors representing hills, roads, and telephone poles that enable AI models to understand what a machine is “seeing.”</p>

<h2 dir="ltr">How the cloud could help autonomous machines</h2>
<p dir="ltr"><a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/self-driving-vehicles/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Autonomous cars</a> are an obvious example. I don&#8217;t think manufacturers will ever replace onboard navigation systems entirely. There are real-time decisions that need to be made through the use of high-definition sensors such as lidar.</p>
<p dir="ltr">However, we can improve decision-making if we know certain things in advance. For example, imagine a future where a last-mile <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/delivery-robots/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">delivery</a> company struggles to consistently transport fresh food in a timely manner due to confusion about the physical world.</p>
<p dir="ltr">In rural areas, <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/self-driving-vehicles/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">autonomous vehicles</a> may fail to recognize that long driveways are often entrances to recipients’ homes. Or, picture a situation within a city, where self-driving cars can’t find a particular apartment within a large complex.</p>
<p dir="ltr">It’s for these reasons that fleet companies use AI and cloud-based tech to create finely detailed and ever-evolving maps of these areas and then serve this information back to the delivery systems. Doing so will allow autonomous vehicles, as well as the couriers who step out of them to hand packages to customers or put them on doorsteps, to speed up delivery times. They could also reduce carbon emissions as well as the risk of taking a wrong turn and getting into an accident.</p>

<h2 dir="ltr">Maps help drones with BVLOS flights</h2>
<p dir="ltr">The U.S. Department of Transportation, through the Federal Aviation Administration, in August <a href="https://apnews.com/article/drones-trump-duffy-line-of-sight-rule-bdbc54ca3b8ef2ead9ccfc62f3762f4c" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-saferedirecturl="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://apnews.com/article/drones-trump-duffy-line-of-sight-rule-bdbc54ca3b8ef2ead9ccfc62f3762f4c&amp;source=gmail&amp;ust=1762646807705000&amp;usg=AOvVaw02KkoGGB7vaNr9O9sNmKij">proposed</a> allowing drones to operate beyond the visual line of sight (<a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/bvlos/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BVLOS</a>) of an operator without needing individual waivers. This would be a significant simplification compared with the current system.</p>
<p dir="ltr">In a future where partially or fully autonomous <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/uav-drones/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">drones</a> operate at scale, delivery companies will need to build and maintain high-resolution maps of the earth that are spatially aware of things like power lines, building shapes and protrusions or other physical-world obstacles.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Power lines and utility poles, in particular, are a significant hazard that drones have to navigate around. And, as is the case with autonomous vehicles that are looking for a recipient’s front door, autonomous drones need to know exactly where on one’s property the recipient wants the package left.</p>
<p dir="ltr">For instance, a high-fidelity machine intelligence-ready map would help a drone to decipher whether a long, narrow shape is a front porch or a swimming pool.</p>

<h2 dir="ltr">Autonomous tractors harvest, share data</h2>
<p dir="ltr">Tractor companies, including <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/john-deere/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">John Deere</a>, have made a lot of progress in the area of autonomy. In 2022, Deere rolled out its first tractor that can work 24 hours a day without a human operator in the cab. These vehicles also address the labor shortage that <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/markets-industries/ag/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">farmers</a> are facing.</p>
<p dir="ltr">As Jahmy Hindman, chief technology officer at Deere, stated at the vehicle’s rollout, &#8220;The last time agriculture was on the precipice of this much change was when we were on the cusp of replacing the horse and plow.”</p>
<p dir="ltr">The <a href="https://www.agriculture.com/news/technology/john-deere-unveils-fully-autonomous-8r-tractor" target="_blank" rel="noopener" data-saferedirecturl="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.agriculture.com/news/technology/john-deere-unveils-fully-autonomous-8r-tractor&amp;source=gmail&amp;ust=1762646807705000&amp;usg=AOvVaw17y42TKEqXYvAgz0PmOfvZ">Deere’s 8R tractor</a> has GPS guidance and incorporates onboard AI and machine learning capabilities. However, tractor manufacturers could take things a step further. These autonomous machines could also tap into detailed maps of their fields.</p>
<p dir="ltr">This is an area where software company, <a href="https://withleaf.io/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Leaf Agriculture</a>, is making a difference. Leaf’s platform connects with data providers such as John Deere, Climate Fieldview, and CNHi among others.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Using Wherobots, Leaf translates the proprietary files from these data providers into a consistent format, making it easy for farmers to define spatial boundaries within their land plot known as “management zones.” Each zone has unique needs due to varying characteristics such as elevation, soil type, slope, and drainage capabilities.</p>
<p dir="ltr">With continuously updated maps showing the management zone they’re in, autonomous tractors can make important, real-time decisions, such as knowing when to adjust or stop spraying, allowing farmers to protect margins in a notoriously low-margin business.</p>
<p dir="ltr">The future of autonomy won’t be defined solely by onboard technology, but rather, by the fusion of real-time machine learning at the edge with rich, cloud-based spatial intelligence. Whether it’s a delivery van navigating a large apartment complex, a drone avoiding power lines, or a tractor adjusting inputs by management zone, the common thread is that autonomous machines perform best when they see beyond their immediate sensors to their broader surroundings.</p>

<p>ACCES I/O Products Inc. this week released the mPCIe-DAAI16-8F, a high-performance PCI Express Mini Card (mPCIe) multifunction analog output/input module. The company said the module delivers 16-bit precision, simultaneous analog output and input, and flexible digital I/O.</p>

<p>Robots are not limited to East Asian manufacturers, American warehouses, or European healthcare providers. Humanoid last month said it has partnered with QSS AI &amp; Robotics to accelerate the development, manufacturing, and deployment of humanoid robots across Saudi Arabia.</p>
<div id="attachment_585939" style="width: 160px" class="wp-caption alignleft"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-585939" class="size-thumbnail wp-image-585939" src="https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Artem-Sokolov_CEO-and-Founder-at-Humanoid-copy-150x150.jpg" alt="Artem Sokolov, founder and CEO of Humanoid" width="150" height="150" srcset="https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Artem-Sokolov_CEO-and-Founder-at-Humanoid-copy-150x150.jpg 150w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Artem-Sokolov_CEO-and-Founder-at-Humanoid-copy-300x300.jpg 300w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Artem-Sokolov_CEO-and-Founder-at-Humanoid-copy-768x768.jpg 768w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Artem-Sokolov_CEO-and-Founder-at-Humanoid-copy-238x238.jpg 238w, https://www.therobotreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Artem-Sokolov_CEO-and-Founder-at-Humanoid-copy.jpg 770w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 150px) 100vw, 150px" /><p id="caption-attachment-585939" class="wp-caption-text">Artem Sokolov, founder and CEO of Humanoid</p></div>
<p>“<span class="il">Saudi</span> Arabia represents one of the most forward-looking markets in the world when it comes to innovation,” stated Artem Sokolov, founder of Humanoid. “This partnership is our first step into the MENA [Middle East and North Africa] region, a market with immense potential for large-scale adoption of humanoid robots. Together with QSS, we aim to bring humanoid robotics from concept to reality, driving efficiency, safety, and progress across industries.&#8221;</p>
<p>The partners plan to localize technologies under Saudi Arabia&#8217;s <a href="https://www.pif.gov.sa/en/private-sector-hub/leadership-vision/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Vision 2030</a> digital transformation strategy. QSS will explore production and support for Humanoid at its Robotics Factory in Riyadh, and the companies have a non-binding, pre-order framework of up to 10,000 <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/robots-platforms/humanoids/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">humanoid</a> robots over the next five years.</p>
<p>QSS and London-based <a href="https://thehumanoid.ai/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Humanoid</a>, formally known as SKL Robotics Ltd., also plan to establish a &#8220;Humanoid Lounge&#8221; in Riyadh to showcase their latest platforms.</p>
<h2>QSS CEO explains initiative to localize humanoids</h2>
<p><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/dr-elie-metri-8aa43b90/?originalSubdomain=sa" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Dr. Elie Metri</a>, CEO of QSS AI &amp; Robotics, replied to the following questions from <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>The Robot Report</em></a> about the collaboration:</p>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation"><strong>Where will Humanoid&#8217;s robots to be used in <span class="il">Saudi</span> Arabia be manufactured &#8212; is your <a href="https://qltyss.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">company</a> just doing assembly and distribution?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Metri:</strong> We are not just assembling or distributing; we are localizing. The humanoid robots that will be deployed in <span class="il">Saudi</span> Arabia will be locally manufactured, integrated, and supported through QSS’s robotics facilities in Riyadh and our factory in Sudair Industrial City.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Our role is to ensure that humanoid robotics becomes part of <span class="il">Saudi</span> Arabia’s industrial base, not just imported technology. This includes local assembly lines, mechanical and electronic subsystem manufacturing, software integration, and field deployment and lifecycle support.</p>
<p dir="ltr">This partnership is about building long-term national capability, not just importing robots.</p>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation"><strong>Can you give examples of the localized customizations that QSS is doing?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Metri:</strong> We are customizing for:</p>
<ul>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">Arabic language and dialects (GCC and <span class="il">Saudi</span>-specific speech models)</li>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">Cultural behavior patterns (gestures, social cues, proximity norms)</li>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">Physical adaptations for climate, such as high heat and dust tolerance</li>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">Industry-specific tool attachments (for manufacturing, retail logistics, hospitality, etc.)</li>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">Compliance with <span class="il">Saudi</span> safety and industrial standards</li>
</ul>
<p dir="ltr">Humanoids must feel native to the environment they work in socially, technically, and operationally.</p>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation"><strong>Will Humanoid be obtaining data in <span class="il">Saudi</span> Arabia to train <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/design-development/ai-cognition/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">artificial intelligence</a> models and further refine its robots? Are there regulatory or privacy concerns?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Metri:</strong> Yes, AI performance improves with real operational data, but data sovereignty is non-negotiable in <span class="il">Saudi</span> Arabia. All data will be captured, processed, and stored locally, governed under <span class="il">Saudi</span> data privacy and cybersecurity regulations.</p>
<p>It will be managed under frameworks aligned with <a href="https://sdaia.gov.sa/en/default.aspx" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SDAIA</a> [the Saudi Data &amp; AI Authority] and <a href="https://sdaia.gov.sa/en/Sectors/NDMO/Pages/default.aspx" target="_blank" rel="noopener">NDMO</a>, the National Data Management Office. We are extremely careful to ensure transparency, auditability, and ethical use of data.</p>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation"><strong>Is QSS hiring or scaling up to support this deployment of 10,000 robots over the next five years?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Metri:</strong> Yes, this scale requires talent and infrastructure expansion. QSS is currently expanding our robotics manufacturing <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/workforce/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">workforce</a> and building new specialization tracks in humanoid mechatronics, AI control systems, and autonomous operations.</p>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation">We&#8217;re also collaborating with <span class="il">Saudi</span> universities and training centers for local talent development. This is not only a robotics rollout; it is job creation and skills localization in line with <span class="il">Saudi</span> Vision 2030.</p>

<h2 dir="ltr">Humanoid and QSS get ready to roll out robots</h2>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation"><strong>What are some of the key performance indicators (KPIs) that the partners expect?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Metri:</strong> We measure success in clear operational terms:</p>
<ul>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">Reduction in repetitive labor and operational overhead</li>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">Consistency and accuracy of task execution</li>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">System uptime and reliability metrics</li>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">Response time for support and maintenance</li>
<li dir="ltr" role="presentation">Overall return on automation investment</li>
</ul>
<p dir="ltr">Humanoid robots must improve operational efficiency, not just look futuristic.</p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Are you already working with local <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/markets-industries/retail-wholesale/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">retailers</a>, <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/markets-industries/manufacturing/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">manufacturers</a>, and other customers?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Metri:</strong> We are already in advanced engagement and pilot planning with major retail chains, manufacturing plants, infrastructure operators, and <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/markets-industries/logistics-warehousing-asrs/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">logistics</a> and <a href="https://www.automatedwarehouseonline.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">warehouse</a> facilities.</p>
<p dir="ltr">The demand signals are very real, especially where there are labor shortages in repetitive and physically intensive roles.</p>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation"><strong>Will the systems be provided through a robotics-as-a-service (<a href="https://www.automatedwarehouseonline.com/category/raas/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">RaaS</a>) model?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Metri:</strong> Yes. RaaS reduces the barrier to adoption and allows organizations to scale usage based on operational needs.</p>
<p dir="ltr">We provide the robot, the software, maintenance and support, and continuous upgrades &#8212; all under a predictable subscription model included in the price of the subscription. The exact prices can be disclosed now.</p>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation"><strong>How soon do the partners expect to deploy humanoid robots in the Middle East?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Metri:</strong> Deployment starts this year through phased pilots in manufacturing, logistics, and infrastructure environments. We are not talking about the distant future; we are already in execution mode.</p>

<h2 dir="ltr">Saudi Arabia aims for robotics leadership</h2>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation"><strong>Is the <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/government/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">government</a> providing any incentives? What are some of the <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/tag/safety" target="_blank" rel="noopener">safety</a> considerations?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><span class="il"><strong>Metri:</strong> Saudi</span> Arabia strongly supports localization of future technologies, and robotics manufacturing is strategically aligned with national priorities.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Safety is core to deployment. Robots will operate under <a href="https://www.iso.org/standard/73933.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ISO 10218</a> and <a href="https://www.iso.org/standard/62996.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ISO 15066</a> safety principles. <a href="https://www.therobotreport.com/category/design-development/haptics/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Human-robot interaction</a> zones are defined and monitored, and continuous fail-safe and emergency stop features are built in. We are deploying robots responsibly and transparently.</p>
<p dir="ltr" role="presentation"><strong>How could this be a model for other humanoid robot deployments in other countries or regions?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr"><span class="il"><strong>Metri:</strong> Yes, that it exactly the point. Saudi</span> Arabia is one of the few markets ready to deploy humanoid robotics at scale, with capital, infrastructure, and national alignment to future industries.</p>
<p dir="ltr">The playbook we are building here &#8212; localization, training, industrial use-cases, and regulatory frameworks &#8212; can be replicated across regions.</p>
